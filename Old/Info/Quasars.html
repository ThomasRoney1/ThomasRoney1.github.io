About Quasars

Quasars, or quasi-stellar objects, are among the most energetic and luminous objects in the universe. They are powered by supermassive black holes at the centers of distant galaxies. As matter falls into these black holes, it heats up and emits vast amounts of electromagnetic radiation, outshining the entire galaxy around them.

Quasars are typically found billions of light-years away, making them important tools for understanding the early universe. Their extreme brightness allows them to be seen across cosmic distances, acting as cosmic lighthouses from the universe's youth.

The energy output from a quasar can be 100 to 1,000 times greater than that of the Milky Way. Despite their brightness, they are small compared to a galaxy, usually no larger than our Solar System in diameter. Their discovery in the 1960s revolutionized astronomy and led to the understanding of active galactic nuclei.

Today, astronomers continue to study quasars to explore black hole growth, galaxy evolution, and the structure of the early universe. Their light also serves as a backlight for observing intergalactic gas and the large-scale structure of space.
