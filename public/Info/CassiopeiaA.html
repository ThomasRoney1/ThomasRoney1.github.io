The glowing remains of a stellar explosion in our galaxy.

About Cassiopeia A

Cassiopeia A is one of the most studied supernova remnants in the Milky Way galaxy. Located about 11,000 light-years from Earth in the constellation Cassiopeia, it is the expanding cloud of debris left behind by a massive star that exploded roughly 350 years ago.

Though the light from the explosion was likely obscured by interstellar dust at the time, the remnant glows brightly in X-rays and radio waves today. Observations from NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory have revealed intricate details of the remnant’s composition, shockwaves, and the compact neutron star at its core.

Cassiopeia A offers astronomers a close-up look at the end stages of stellar evolution. Its structure includes filaments of hot gas and heavy elements like oxygen, sulfur, and iron — the building blocks of future stars and planets.

Studying Cassiopeia A provides critical insight into the physics of supernovae, the origins of cosmic rays, and the life cycle of matter in the galaxy. It remains a key target for both space- and ground-based telescopes.
