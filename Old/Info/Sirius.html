The brightest star in Earth's night sky, located in the constellation Canis Major.

About Sirius

Sirius, often called the "Dog Star," is the brightest star visible from Earth. It is part of the constellation Canis Major and is located approximately 8.6 light-years from Earth. Sirius is a binary star system, consisting of Sirius A, a main-sequence star, and Sirius B, a white dwarf.

Sirius A is around 25 times more luminous than our Sun and has about twice its mass. It has an apparent magnitude of -1.46, making it 25 times more luminous than the Sun. Its extreme brightness and proximity to Earth make it easily visible, even in light-polluted areas.

Sirius has been known since ancient times and holds significant cultural importance. In ancient Egypt, the heliacal rising of Sirius marked the beginning of the annual flooding of the Nile River, which was crucial for agriculture. In modern astronomy, it is used to help calibrate distance measurements and serves as a primary target for stellar study.

Over the course of its long life, Sirius will eventually exhaust its hydrogen fuel and become a red giant before shedding its outer layers to form a planetary nebula. Sirius B, the white dwarf, will remain behind as a dense, faint remnant.
