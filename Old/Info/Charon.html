Charon

Discover Pluto’s largest moon and its deep connection with the dwarf planet.

About Charon

Charon is the largest moon of Pluto and is so massive relative to its parent that they are sometimes referred to as a binary system. Discovered in 1978, Charon is about half the size of Pluto and shares a unique orbital relationship, with both bodies tidally locked to one another.

The surface of Charon is a mix of water ice, deep canyons, and mysterious reddish regions near its poles. The red areas are thought to be tholins—complex organic molecules—transferred from Pluto's escaping atmosphere and chemically altered by ultraviolet light.

NASA's New Horizons mission provided the first detailed images of Charon in 2015, revealing towering cliffs and a vast depression that may be the remains of an ancient ocean beneath its icy crust.

Scientists continue to study Charon for clues about the history of the outer solar system and the formation of Pluto-Charon as a possible captured binary.
