Enceladus

Saturn's icy moon, hiding a vast ocean beneath its frozen surface.

About Enceladus

Enceladus is one of Saturn’s most intriguing moons, known for its bright icy surface and active geysers. Measuring about 504 kilometers (313 miles) across, it is relatively small but captures immense scientific interest due to the presence of a global subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust.

NASA's Cassini spacecraft discovered plumes of water vapor and ice erupting from Enceladus’s south pole, suggesting hydrothermal activity on the ocean floor. These plumes contain organic molecules, hinting that Enceladus could potentially harbor life.

The surface of Enceladus is a mixture of smooth plains, craters, and complex fractures known as "tiger stripes." These stripes are the source of the moon's powerful geysers, spraying material hundreds of kilometers into space and feeding Saturn’s E ring.

Despite being so far from the Sun, tidal heating caused by gravitational interactions with Saturn and neighboring moons generates enough energy to maintain the internal ocean in a liquid state. Scientists view Enceladus as one of the most promising places in our Solar System to search for extraterrestrial life.

Future missions to explore Enceladus could reveal more about its ocean’s chemistry, the possibility of microbial life, and the history of this icy world.
