Deimos

The smaller of Mars' two moons, with a mysterious origin.

About Deimos

Deimos is the smaller of Mars' two moons, orbiting the red planet at a distance of about 23,460 kilometers. It is one of the smallest moons in the Solar System and is irregularly shaped, measuring approximately 12.4 kilometers across at its widest point.

Deimos is believed to be a captured asteroid, likely originating from the asteroid belt. Its surface is covered in a layer of fine dust and debris, with craters scattered across its surface. Its low gravity and irregular orbit suggest that it was once a part of the early Solar System, drawn in by Mars' gravity millions of years ago.

Deimos' orbit is quite eccentric, meaning it is slowly moving away from Mars. Unlike its larger counterpart, Phobos, Deimos does not exhibit significant tidal forces that could cause it to crash into Mars in the distant future. This has led scientists to speculate that Deimos may remain in orbit for billions of years.

Despite its small size, Deimos holds significance in the study of planetary moons. It is thought to be one of the best-preserved examples of a captured asteroid, and studying it provides valuable insights into the processes of celestial body capture, gravitational interaction, and the history of Mars.
