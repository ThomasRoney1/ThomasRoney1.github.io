About Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life. With a rich atmosphere, vast oceans, and diverse ecosystems, Earth has developed unique conditions for the flourishing of life. Its surface is made up of approximately 71% water and 29% land, and it features a variety of biomes, including forests, deserts, and polar ice caps.

Earth orbits the Sun at an average distance of 149.6 million kilometers, taking roughly 365.25 days to complete one revolution. This rotation gives rise to day and night, while the axial tilt creates the seasons. Earth's magnetic field, generated by the movement of molten iron in its core, protects the planet from harmful solar radiation.

Earth has a natural satellite, the Moon, which is responsible for phenomena such as tides. The relationship between Earth and the Moon has been central to understanding the dynamics of celestial bodies in our solar system. Earth's history has been marked by dramatic geological changes, including plate tectonics, volcanic activity, and the shifting of continents.

Earth is currently facing significant environmental challenges, including climate change, deforestation, and pollution. Efforts to mitigate these issues are critical to ensuring the planet's continued habitability for future generations.
