About Callisto

Callisto is the second-largest moon of Jupiter and the third-largest moon in the solar system. Discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, it is one of the Galilean moons and is notable for its ancient, heavily cratered surface that dates back billions of years.

With a diameter of about 4,821 kilometers, Callisto is almost as large as the planet Mercury. Its surface is the oldest and most heavily cratered in the solar system, lacking tectonic or volcanic activity, which suggests it has been geologically inactive for a long time.

Beneath its icy crust, scientists believe Callisto may harbor a subsurface ocean, raising the possibility of microbial life. It also has a very thin atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide and possibly molecular oxygen.

Callisto’s distant orbit around Jupiter means it's less affected by the planet’s radiation belts, making it a potential candidate for future human outposts in space. Its stark beauty and mysterious interior continue to be subjects of interest for planetary science.
