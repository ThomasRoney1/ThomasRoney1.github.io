Venus Surface

An intensely hot and volcanic world shrouded by thick clouds of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid.

About Venus Surface

The surface of Venus is one of the most hostile environments in the solar system. Temperatures soar to around 900°F (475°C), hotter than the surface of Mercury, despite Venus being farther from the Sun. This is due to a runaway greenhouse effect fueled by its thick atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide.

Venus’s surface is rocky and volcanic, dominated by vast plains, towering mountains, and numerous volcanoes. Features such as Maxwell Montes — the tallest mountain on Venus — and extensive lava flows hint at a geologically active past.

The planet's thick, toxic clouds obscure its surface from optical view, requiring radar mapping from missions like NASA’s Magellan spacecraft to reveal its detailed topography. The high pressure at the surface, about 92 times that of Earth’s, would crush most human-made spacecraft within hours.

Despite these extreme conditions, Venus remains a key target for future exploration as scientists seek to understand its climatic history, volcanic activity, and the possibilities of microbial life in its upper atmosphere.
