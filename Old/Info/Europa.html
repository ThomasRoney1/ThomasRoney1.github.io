Europa

A moon of Jupiter with a hidden ocean beneath its icy surface.

About Europa

Europa is one of Jupiter's largest moons and is of particular interest to scientists due to its potential to harbor life beneath its icy surface. Discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, it is primarily composed of water ice, with a smooth, icy surface covering a subsurface ocean that may be in contact with a rocky mantle, making it a prime candidate for astrobiological research.

Europa's surface is crisscrossed with dark lines and cracks, which are believed to be the result of tidal flexing caused by Jupiter’s immense gravitational pull. These cracks suggest that the icy crust is floating on a subsurface ocean, which may be kept liquid by the heat generated through tidal forces.

The idea that Europa could harbor life is fueled by evidence of chemical ingredients necessary for life, such as organic compounds, and the presence of liquid water beneath its surface. Scientists are particularly interested in the potential for hydrothermal vents at the bottom of Europa's ocean, where life could exist without the need for sunlight.

Europa has been a target for exploration by both spacecraft and proposed future missions, such as NASA’s Europa Clipper mission, which will conduct detailed reconnaissance of the moon’s ice shell and subsurface ocean. Its intriguing surface features and the possibility of life make Europa one of the most fascinating bodies in our solar system.
