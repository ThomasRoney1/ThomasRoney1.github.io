About Polaris

Polaris, also known as the North Star or Alpha Ursae Minoris, is one of the most famous stars in the night sky. Located nearly directly above Earth's northern celestial pole, Polaris has served as a celestial beacon for navigators and explorers for centuries.

Contrary to popular belief, Polaris is not a single star but a multiple star system composed of a supergiant primary and at least two smaller companions. The primary star, Polaris A, is a yellow-white supergiant about 2,500 times more luminous than our Sun and approximately 433 light-years away from Earth.

Polaris appears almost fixed in the sky due to its alignment with the Earth’s axis of rotation. As Earth spins, the stars seem to rotate around Polaris, making it an essential point of reference for celestial navigation in the Northern Hemisphere.

While it hasn't always been the North Star, and won’t always remain so due to the precession of Earth’s axis, Polaris currently holds a unique and culturally significant place in astronomy, navigation, and history.
