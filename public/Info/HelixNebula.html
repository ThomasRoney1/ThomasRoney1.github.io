Helix Nebula

A stunning planetary nebula, often referred to as the "Eye of God."

About Helix Nebula

The Helix Nebula, also known as NGC 7293, is a planetary nebula located approximately 700 light-years from Earth in the constellation Aquarius. It is one of the closest planetary nebulae to Earth and is often referred to as the "Eye of God" due to its resemblance to a human eye when viewed through a telescope.

The nebula is the result of a dying star, a red giant, shedding its outer layers. This process exposes the star’s hot core, which emits ultraviolet light that ionizes the surrounding gas, causing it to glow in a wide range of colors. The Helix Nebula spans about 2,500 light-years across, making it one of the largest known planetary nebulae.

The central star of the nebula, which is in the process of becoming a white dwarf, has a surface temperature of around 100,000°C. This intense heat is responsible for the nebula's bright and colorful appearance. The Helix Nebula is a prime example of a late stage in stellar evolution, showing the transition of a star from a red giant to a white dwarf.

The nebula's distinct structure is formed by the ejection of gas in layers, creating a unique, intricate pattern. The nebula also contains a wealth of interesting features, including bright knots of gas, dark lanes of dust, and intricate filaments of ionized gas, all of which have been captured in stunning detail by telescopes like the Hubble Space Telescope.

The Helix Nebula provides valuable insights into the life cycle of stars, especially those of low to medium mass like our Sun. As stars like the Sun approach the end of their lives, they will expel their outer layers and become planetary nebulae, leaving behind a hot core that eventually cools to become a white dwarf.
