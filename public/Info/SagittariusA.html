Sagittarius A*

The supermassive black hole at the heart of the Milky Way Galaxy.

About Sagittarius A*

Sagittarius A* (pronounced "Sagittarius A-star") is a compact, extremely bright source of radio waves located at the very center of the Milky Way Galaxy. It is believed to be a supermassive black hole, with a mass approximately 4 million times that of our Sun.

This galactic centerpiece lies about 26,000 light-years from Earth in the direction of the constellation Sagittarius. Although it cannot be directly observed in visible light due to thick clouds of gas and dust, astronomers use radio, X-ray, and infrared observations to study its properties.

The first compelling evidence for Sagittarius A*’s existence came from decades of tracking the orbits of stars near the galactic center, particularly one known as S2. These observations showed that an enormous, invisible mass was influencing the stars — consistent with a black hole.

In 2022, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first image of Sagittarius A*, revealing the black hole’s shadow and glowing ring of gas. This landmark achievement confirmed long-standing theories about black holes and strengthened our understanding of galactic dynamics.
