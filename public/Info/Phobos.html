Phobos

Mars' larger and innermost moon, racing around its planet with astonishing speed.

About Phobos

Phobos is the larger and closer of Mars’ two natural moons, the other being Deimos. Discovered in 1877 by American astronomer Asaph Hall, Phobos is irregularly shaped and heavily cratered, resembling a captured asteroid rather than a typical moon.

It orbits Mars at an incredibly close distance of about 6,000 kilometers (3,700 miles) above the surface — closer than any other moon in the Solar System relative to its planet. Because of this, Phobos races around Mars, completing an orbit roughly every 7.6 hours, faster than Mars' own rotation.

Phobos is gradually spiraling inward toward Mars at a rate of about 2 centimeters per year. In roughly 30 to 50 million years, it is expected to either crash into Mars or break apart, forming a ring around the planet.

The surface of Phobos is covered in a thick layer of regolith (dust and rock) and is dominated by a large impact crater named Stickney, which is nearly half the moon’s diameter. Scientists believe that studying Phobos can provide valuable insight into Mars’ past and the early Solar System.
