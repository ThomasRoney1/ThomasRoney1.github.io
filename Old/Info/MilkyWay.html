About the Milky Way

The Milky Way Galaxy is a barred spiral galaxy that houses our Solar System, along with billions of other stars, planets, and cosmic structures. Spanning approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter, it is home to an estimated 100–400 billion stars and perhaps even more planets.

The galaxy has a central bulge dominated by older, redder stars, surrounded by four major spiral arms filled with young, hot stars, gas, and dust. At the heart of the Milky Way lies Sagittarius A*, a supermassive black hole with millions of times the mass of our Sun.

Viewed from Earth, the Milky Way appears as a dense, milky band stretching across the night sky — a view resulting from our position inside the disk, looking edge-on through its thickest parts. The name "Milky Way" derives from its appearance as a dim "milky" glowing band.

The galaxy is surrounded by a halo of older stars and globular clusters, along with an even larger, invisible halo of dark matter, crucial for holding the structure together. Over billions of years, the Milky Way has absorbed smaller galaxies and continues to interact gravitationally with its cosmic neighbors, including the Andromeda Galaxy.

Astronomers study the Milky Way to understand galaxy formation, stellar evolution, and the overall structure of the universe. Every new discovery within our galactic home adds another piece to the cosmic puzzle of our origins.
