Ganymede

The largest moon in our Solar System, a world of ice and mystery.

About Ganymede

Ganymede is the largest moon of Jupiter and the largest moon in the entire solar system, even larger than the planet Mercury. It was discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, along with three other major moons of Jupiter, now collectively known as the Galilean moons.

Ganymede is unique in many ways. Its icy surface is characterized by both bright and dark regions, with the bright areas composed of water ice and the dark regions being rocky. Beneath its surface, Ganymede is believed to have an iron-nickel core, surrounded by a thick mantle of ice and a subsurface ocean of liquid water. This makes Ganymede one of the few places in our solar system where conditions might be suitable for life.

Ganymede also stands out due to its magnetic field, which is the only one known to be generated by a moon, possibly by a liquid iron core. The moon's magnetic field interacts with Jupiter's powerful magnetosphere, creating unique auroras near its poles.

Exploration of Ganymede has been limited, but it has been studied by spacecraft like NASA's Galileo and more recently by the Juno mission. Ganymede is a key target for future exploration, including potential missions such as the European Space Agency's Jupiter Icy Moons Explorer (JUICE), which will focus on studying Ganymede, Europa, and Callisto in greater detail.
